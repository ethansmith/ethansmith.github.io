How it begins

A meal. And then another.

Tribal fishers brought salmon. Farmers brought potatoes and berries. A Lummi leader shared his lineage back to the 1855 treaty. A fourth-generation farmer said it was the first time he had ever sat down to eat with a tribal member. By the end of the evening, something had shifted — not solved, not fixed, just opened.

That opening is the work. Plans do not create moments like that. Policies do not either. People do, when someone creates the conditions for it — slowly, intentionally, at the speed of trust.

Since 2023, the project has brought together more than 150 Lummi and Nooksack tribal members, Punjabi and multigenerational Anglo farmers, civic and NGO leaders, and neighbors from across Whatcom County for gatherings along the full length of the river.

What happens in the room is documented through film, photography, fine art, writing, and immersive installation — reflected back to participants and the wider public in near real time. Eight short films are complete or in production. A portrait series by Bellingham artist Christopher Remmers is growing. A Western Washington University StoryMap lets anyone listen from five points along the river.

Why this matters now

The window is open. Briefly.

This summer, Washington state agencies are convening roundtables on chronic water stress. This project has been invited to help design, convene, and facilitate one of the Washington's Water Future roundtables — a moment that requires community trust to already be in the room.

The Colorado River was divided for over a century with almost no meaningful cross-sector relationship-building. By the time the water ran short, there was no shared trust to draw on. The Yakima basin spent decades in expensive litigation before the coalition needed for a coordinated plan began to form. Farmers lost security. Tribes lost fish. Communities paid a price they could not recover.

The Nooksack does not have to follow that path. This work is about the shift from scarcity to abundance — not a better legal mechanism for dividing a shrinking resource, but the relationships and shared vision required to grow something larger together.

The investment case

What $5M can unlock

After years of facilitated relationship-building, the Yakima basin produced something no courtroom could: a unified coalition that opened the door to a multi-decade integrated plan and hundreds of millions in federal and state investment. Policymakers consistently fund coalitions of tribes and farmers arriving with a credible shared vision. The rare thing is the relational work required to make that coalition possible.

Who we are

A four-person team with roots in this place

The project is led by four people with deep roots in Whatcom County. Aaron Straight (CEO/Founder) carries the originating vision and leads film direction and community relationships. Jeff Bos (CFO) designs and facilitates the community convenings; his funding for this phase comes from a separately procured grant. Ethan Smith (COO) shapes strategy, partnerships, and external communications. Christopher Remmers (CCO) directs the initiative's visual and artistic language across all media.

The project is currently fiscally sponsored by Whatcom Community Foundation. Assuming H2 2026 fundraising succeeds, the team plans to form an independent entity by the end of 2026.

The role is not to make policy or litigate. It is to convene, document, and help hold the relationships this work requires.
