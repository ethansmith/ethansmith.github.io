The Work

Something opened that evening

Tribal fishers brought salmon. Farmers brought potatoes and berries. A Lummi leader shared his lineage back to the 1855 treaty. A fourth-generation farmer said it was the first time he had ever sat down to eat with a tribal member.

By the end of the evening, something had shifted — not resolved, not fixed, just opened. That opening is the work. Plans don't create moments like that. Policies don't. People do, when someone creates the conditions for it — slowly, intentionally, at the speed of trust.

Water Wars (how to avoid) is a storytelling and relationship-building project working to cultivate what scholar Otto Scharmer calls the "social soil" of the Nooksack River and adjacent watersheds — the relational ground from which any durable future must grow.

Since 2023, the project has brought together over 150 Lummi and Nooksack tribal members, Punjabi and multigenerational Anglo farmers, civic and NGO leaders, and multigenerational neighbors from across Whatcom County — from the river's headwaters on Nooksack territory to family farms on its floodplains to the delta on Lummi lands.

Food from this region anchors every gathering. Cooking and eating together is not a perk — it is a practice. The river and the soil show up on the table, and people meet each other across it.

Eight short films. Portrait paintings by Bellingham artist Christopher Remmers. Five published articles, six newsletters, a WWU StoryMap. These are not feel-good convenings. They are strategic investments in the relational infrastructure that any durable future for this watershed requires.

Water Wars (how to avoid) has been invited to help bring people and ideas to that table — and to make clear that the community's work of building trust, shared story, and cross-cutting relationships is already underway.

Why It Matters Now

Two paths. One choice.

The Colorado River was divided among states, cities, and agricultural users for over a century with almost no meaningful cross-sector relationship-building. By the time the water ran short, there was no shared trust to draw on. The Nooksack does not have to follow that path.

The Yakima basin took the other path. Years of relational groundwork produced something no courtroom could: Indigenous leaders and farmers standing together with a shared vision. That opened the door to a multi-decade integrated plan and over $800M in federal and state investment.

From scarcity to abundance. Adjudication assumes a fixed pie and divides the losses. A unified coalition can pursue the large-scale, coordinated investment that makes abundance possible. That shift begins with relationship — not with a plan.

What's less obvious — and just as important — is that this kind of relational infrastructure doesn't only support water stewardship. The same social soil that makes a watershed agreement more durable also makes a community more resilient to economic disruption, ecological crisis, and the broader unraveling of institutional trust.

The Investment Case

$5M in. $400M+ unlocked.

The pattern is established: when tribes and farmers arrive in Washington with a unified vision, investment follows. The Yakima Integrated Water Resource Management Plan — built on exactly this relational foundation — has channeled over $800M in federal and state investment to date, with roughly $60M flowing in every two years. Policymakers nearly always fund coalitions of tribes and farmers. What is rare is someone doing the work to create those conditions before the money is on the table.

Core Team

The value proposition is the team

The team's combined background in documentary filmmaking, large-scale facilitation, strategic systems design, and fine art is not incidental to the project's value. It is the value. These are specialists in exactly the disciplines that contested watershed work requires and that no single agency can replicate.

Aaron Straight
Founder · Lead Storyteller · Documentary Director

Aaron carries the originating vision for Water Wars and holds the deepest continuity with the project's relational and documentary roots. He leads core film direction, shapes the broader story world, and stewards key community relationships across Lummi, Nooksack, and farming communities. His approach is direct, unhurried, and deeply conversational — the quality of listening that makes documentary subjects reveal what they would not say to a journalist. His work has a direct line from the river to the screen and back to the community that needs to see itself in the story.

Jeff Bos
Facilitator · Process Steward · Social Soil Practitioner

Jeff brings decades of cross-sector facilitation experience — including global work in regenerative agriculture, systems change, and community-based foresight — to the design of Water Wars' gathering architecture. He stewards the invisible conditions that make collaboration possible: the room design, the pacing, the permission for silence. He holds the 170-year visioning horizon that gives the project its long arc. His funding from the Novo Foundation anchors the facilitation budget through 2027, representing the initiative's first significant committed investment.

Ethan Smith
Strategy · Container Architecture · Brand Steward

Ethan is the architect of the container that makes Water Wars possible at scale — holding narrative coherence, structural legibility, and the connective tissue between intimate local work and the global movements in regenerative place-stewardship it draws from. He leads strategy and the 18-month community visioning process, owns the initiative's brand and digital presence, and connects Water Wars to international forums and funders. He has lived in this watershed since 2003, kayaked its upper reaches, and is raising his children here. Nearly two decades scaling programs at the Biomimicry Institute, in digital health (concept to $8M federal approval), and in planetary science policy.

Christopher Remmers
Visual Artist · Creative Director · Portrait Painter

Christopher is a Bellingham-based fine artist whose portrait series has become the visual backbone of the project — over a dozen paintings shown in community halls, galleries, and public events across Whatcom County. He shapes the visual and conceptual language of the initiative across media, ensuring that Water Wars' artistic outputs carry the depth needed for both museum and community contexts. Christopher's work is how the project leaves a physical record in the places it moves through.
