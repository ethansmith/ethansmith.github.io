Jay Julius, Lummi Nation, to raspberry farmer Brad Rader — 2023

They had been sitting on opposite sides of one of the most contested water conflicts in Washington State. Something shifted in that conversation. On the recommendation of the Whatcom Community Foundation, the two of them reached out to filmmaker and community convener Aaron Straight with a different question: what if someone told a story of cooperation instead of conflict? What began as a film project has become something far deeper.

The River & What's at Stake

Perfect setup for a battle.
Except something else is happening here.

The Nooksack River flows from glacial peaks to the Salish Sea, keeping everything alive here: the salmon, the tribes, the forests, and for the last couple of hundred years, the farms and cities. Things are changing. The glaciers are shrinking. Rain falls differently. The river carries more water in winter and less in summer, when every user needs it most.

In 2023, the state launched formal water rights adjudication — a legal process to rank water rights by seniority that, in other watersheds, has meant decades of courtroom battles while salmon populations crash and family farms fold.

The Colorado River was carved up for over a century without meaningful cross-sector relationship-building. The Yakima basin spent generations in expensive court battles. Farmers lost security. Tribes lost fish. Communities lost years of environmental, social, and economic health they could not get back.

Those losses are the cost of skipping the step we are now taking.

What Water Wars (how to avoid) Is

More than 150 people. Three years. One river.

Working in the Nooksack watershed since 2023, bringing together Lummi and Nooksack tribal members, multigenerational farmers, civic leaders, and neighbors from the river's headwaters to its delta.

Gathering & Convening

Hosted dinners, river walks, retreats, and unscripted conversations. Food from this region is at the center of every gathering — salmon, clams, and crab from the delta; potatoes, skyr, and berries from Whatcom farms. Cooking and eating together is not a perk. It is a practice.

Film & Documentary

Eight short films complete or in production by filmmaker Aaron Straight of Soulcraft Allstars. We Are Not Enemies (25–30 min) anchors the fall 2026 campaign. Feature documentary A River Runs Through Us begins development in 2027.

Fine Art & Portrait Work

A growing portrait series by Bellingham classical figurative painter Christopher Remmers. Large-scale narrative paintings give a divided community something to stand before together — images of a shared future — and leave a physical record in every place this project moves through.

Public Engagement & Storytelling

A Western Washington University StoryMap invites anyone to stand virtually at five points along the Nooksack and simply listen. Written articles, monthly newsletters, and a fall immersive screening campaign bring the story to theaters, grange halls, libraries, and museums across Whatcom County.

How the Work Works

Not trust first, then action.
A feedback loop.

There is a persistent and costly assumption in how these efforts usually unfold: that trust-building and problem-solving are two separate phases. First get people comfortable, then hand them a challenge to solve. That sequencing is exactly why so many well-engineered solutions fail to stick. The trust was never tested against real work, so it collapses the moment the solution gets hard or contested.

Water Wars runs a different process. Partners — tribal leaders, farmers, agencies — work through defining problems together, building solutions together, and learning from what happens when those solutions meet reality.

This work will not stop at water. The relationships being built here are the same ones needed to work together on landscape stewardship, economic resilience, and whatever hard problems come next. Water is the entry point. What gets built around the table is not water-specific. It is a community that knows how to navigate hard problems together.

The Loop

Each round of the cycle does two things at once: it produces better solutions, and it deepens the trust between the people who have to live with them. That trust carries everyone into the next, harder problem. A feedback loop, not a precondition.

"We don't build trust and then solve problems — we facilitate people through actually understanding the problem and building solutions together, and it's that shared work, done well, that produces the trust needed to tackle the next, harder problem."

— Jeff Bos, Facilitator

Why This Matters Now

The roundtables are this summer. We are ready.

This summer, Washington state agencies are convening roundtables on chronic water stress, competing needs, and long-term solutions. Water Wars has been invited to participate — and to demonstrate that something different is already underway here.

The work of building trust across traditional divides, of walking the river together and sharing stories across difference, has been happening with or without state involvement. What we are bringing to those tables is not a set of solutions or demands. It is the beginning of a coalition, and a signal that the people of this watershed are ready to do the harder, longer work together.

The 170-year vision process at the center of our strategy looks backward to the Point Elliott Treaty of 1855, signed just 170 years ago, and forward to the world our children's grandchildren will inherit. The answer, co-created by Lummi and Nooksack peoples, farmers, environmentalists, cities, and communities across the watershed, will animate everything that follows: the strategy, the milestones, and ultimately a shared vision strong enough to unlock large-scale funding from Olympia and Washington DC.

The structure we are building now is the condition under which serious long-term planning becomes possible — and what we show up with this summer matters not only for the state roundtables, but for every funder, agency partner, and community leader who will need to believe that this basin is ready to do something different.

What other watersheds teach us

Colorado & early Yakima: the cost of skipping this step

The Colorado River was carved up for over a century without meaningful cross-sector relationship-building — leading to billions in legal fees alone. The Yakima basin spent generations in expensive court battles. Farmers lost security. Tribes lost fish. Communities lost years they could not get back.

Yakima today: what becomes possible after

After years of facilitated relationship-building, the Yakima basin produced a unified coalition with a shared vision — opening the door to a multi-decade integrated plan. Roughly $60 million flows into that basin every two years through federal and state investment. Over $800 million to date. We plan to learn from them and model what worked.

The Next Phase

Here is the work between now and Washington DC.

Picture eighteen months from now. A Lummi fisher, a local dairy producer, a civic leader, and a land steward arrive in Washington DC together — not because they are out of options, but because they have eaten together, walked the same river, and watched each other's stories on a screen in a grange hall. They carry a joint position on the future of the Nooksack. They also carry a film.

Phase 1 — Now through 2027 Community Visioning & Coalition Building

Key leaders from Indigenous, farming, and civic communities continue meeting through retreats, hosted dinners, river walks, and unscripted conversation — the kind of encounters where trust actually forms. Locally-sourced food remains at the center. Film, art, and writing carry what surfaces in those rooms forward. A two-day Yakima Valley field trip, coordinated with Water for Whatcom, serves as both a learning trip and part of the story work.

Starting in late October, a traveling immersive experience moves across Whatcom County — theaters, grange halls, libraries, school auditoriums, and museums. New and existing films, Christopher Remmers' portrait paintings, projected river footage, and a watershed soundscape weave into an evening of story, encounter, and collective imagination. A flagship event at Pickford Cinema and Spark Museum anchors the Bellingham campaign. A targeted online campaign extends the invitation countywide. These are not just screenings. Each one gathers voices, stories, and visions from people who will never sit in a closed leadership meeting but still belong in the future being shaped here.

A 12 to 18-month process — the most ambitious thing this project has attempted. A sustained series of convenings, conversations, and creative experiences built around one question: what kind of future do the people of this watershed want to build together, and what would it take to get there? The process looks backward to the Point Elliott Treaty of 1855 and forward to the world our children's grandchildren will inherit. Three additional short film vignettes feed directly into this process, asking each subject: What do you see for this river and its people in 170 years? Looking back from that future, what stories will they tell of us now?

  • A formal coalition of Lummi, Nooksack, and farming leaders backed by broad community alignment
  • A feature film bringing that vision to life through multiple voices and the accumulated vignette archive
  • A series of large native narrative paintings by Christopher Remmers
  • Stories and spoken-word pieces told from the perspective of that future, in Lummi, in Nooksack, by farmers, and by others across the community
  • A written vision document, which becomes the foundation for Phase 2 strategy
  • A site visit to the Yakima region to learn directly from the people who built that process
  • A clear plan and budget for Phase 2: long-term strategy development
  • Meetings between coalition members and decision makers in DC and Olympia to secure Phase 2 and 3 funding
Phase 2Long-Term Strategy Development

Back-casting from the long-term vision to identify the milestones the community would need to reach. This aligns visionary leaders with the working groups doing detailed planning — connecting the people who see the destination with the people charting the route.

Phase 3Co-managed Watershed Stewardship Plan

A co-managed plan developed by community leaders and subject-matter experts from across relevant agencies and disciplines. Throughout both phases, the Water Wars team continues to convene and cultivate the social soil that makes those formal processes workable — a long-term convening body patterned on what the Yakima process eventually built.

Who We Are

Four people. Over 90 years of combined experience. Deep roots in Whatcom County.

A documentary filmmaker, an innovation and design strategist, a visual artist, and an executive team facilitator — with breadth across program leadership, operations, finance, strategy, community organizing, design, partnership development, and education.

Aaron Straight

Founder · Lead Storyteller · Documentary Director

Aaron carries the originating vision for Water Wars and holds the deepest continuity with the project's relational and documentary roots. As founder of Soulcraft Allstars, a Bellingham-based purpose-driven film studio, he has spent two decades directing films on six continents for clients including the Bill and Melinda Gates Foundation, Lummi Nation, the B Corporation, USDA, Heifer International, Sealaska, and Al Gore — work recognized with Webby, Telly, and Anthem Awards. His approach is direct, unhurried, and deeply conversational — the quality of listening that makes documentary subjects reveal what they would not say to a journalist.

Jeff Bos

Facilitator · Process Steward · Social Soil Practitioner

Jeff brings two decades of cross-sector leadership to the design of Water Wars' gathering architecture, with deep fluency in the food, finance, and agriculture systems that shape how land and water are used. As CFO/COO of the Non-GMO Project he doubled revenues and led a full cultural and strategic transformation; as Chief Growth and Impact Officer at the Soil and Climate Initiative he now works directly with farmers and food companies navigating the transition to regenerative practice. His facilitation lineages span the Presencing Institute's U.Lab, Co-Active Coaching, and Capgemini's Accelerated Solutions Environment — a practice that has guided unlikely collaborations across four continents, in boardrooms, on river banks, in longhouses, and in wilderness.

Ethan Smith

Strategy · Container Architecture · Brand Steward

Ethan holds narrative coherence, structural legibility, and the connective tissue between intimate local work and the global movements in regenerative place-stewardship this project draws from. He brings nearly two decades of cross-sector experience: from connecting millions with nature-inspired innovation as Program Director of AskNature at the Biomimicry Institute, to leading design strategy at Johnson & Johnson and lululemon, to founding ÆVI, a Bellingham-based consultancy at the intersection of systems thinking, biomimicry, and regenerative futures. He leads strategy, owns the brand and digital presence, and connects Water Wars to international forums and funders.

Christopher Remmers

Visual Artist · Creative Director · Experience Curation

Christopher is a Bellingham-based classical figurative oil painter whose portrait and narrative works have become the visual backbone of the project. Over two decades, his paintings have been exhibited internationally, sold at Sotheby's in New York, recognized by the Art Renewal Center and the Beautiful Bizarre Art Prize, and archived in the Lunar Codex — a collection preserved on the Moon. He co-founded the Conscious Creativity Academy and co-created Wander Wild with Jeff Bos, a nature-based immersive program that has moved more than 300 people. In Water Wars, Christopher designs the exhibition experiences that give a divided community something to stand before together.

Water Wars (how to avoid) is fiscally sponsored by Whatcom Community Foundation. Assuming the current fundraising round succeeds, the team plans to form an independent entity by end of 2026. A growing Stewardship Council drawn from Indigenous leadership, agriculture, ecology, law, philanthropy, and civic life across the region supports the work. The role of this team is not to make policy or litigate. It is to convene, document, and hold the relationships this work requires.
