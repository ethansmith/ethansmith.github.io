Water Wars (how to avoid)The Project
How it begins

A meal. And then another.

Tribal fishers brought salmon. Farmers brought potatoes and berries. A Lummi leader shared his lineage back to the 1855 treaty. A fourth-generation farmer said it was the first time he had ever sat down to eat with a tribal member. By the end of the evening, something had shifted. Not solved. Not fixed. Opened.

That opening is the work. Plans do not create moments like that. Policies do not either. People do, when someone takes the time to bring them together and keep showing up.

"The root of most of our problems are relational."

Rich Appel — Independent dairy farmer

Common Waters Gathering — Nooksack River

Since 2023, the project has brought together more than 150 Lummi and Nooksack tribal members, Punjabi and multigenerational Anglo farmers, civic leaders, and neighbors from across Whatcom County — from the river's headwaters to its delta on Lummi lands.

What the project is

Water Wars (how to avoid) is a storytelling and relationship-building project cultivating the conditions for a shared future in the Nooksack River and adjacent watersheds.

Food from this region is part of every gathering. Salmon, clams, and crab from the delta. Potatoes, skyr, and berries from Whatcom farms. Cooking and eating together is not a perk — it is a practice.

The work is documented through film, photography, visual art, and articles. Eight short films are complete or in production. A portrait series by Bellingham artist Christopher Remmers is growing. A Western Washington University StoryMap lets anyone stand virtually at five points along the river and simply listen.

These are not feel-good convenings. They are strategic investments in the relational infrastructure that any durable future for this watershed requires — the kind that no technical plan, by itself, can provide.

Informed by Otto Scharmer's framework for building social soil and enabling emergent futures, the initiative works from the premise that lasting change requires cultivating the conditions for trust before the seeds of any institutional agreement can take root.

Why this matters now

The window is open. Briefly.

This summer, Washington state agencies are convening roundtables on chronic water stress. This project has been invited to help design, convene, and facilitate one of the Washington's Water Future roundtables — a moment that requires community trust to already be in the room.

The lesson from other watersheds is plain: legal and technical frameworks can ratify agreements. They cannot produce the relationships that make agreements hold. Trust has to come first.

When relationship-building fails

The Colorado River was divided for over a century with almost no meaningful cross-sector relationship-building. By the time the water ran short, there was no shared trust to draw on.

The Yakima basin spent decades in expensive litigation before the coalition needed for a coordinated plan began to form. Farmers lost security. Tribes lost fish. Communities paid a price they could not recover. The Nooksack does not have to follow that path.

This work is also about shifting from a scarcity frame — who gets what water — to an abundance frame: what becomes possible when the right people are in long-term relationship with each other and with the river.

The investment case

What $5M can unlock

After years of facilitated relationship-building, the Yakima basin produced something no courtroom could: a unified coalition with a shared vision that opened the door to a multi-decade integrated plan and hundreds of millions in federal and state investment. Policymakers consistently say yes to coalitions of tribes and farmers arriving together. The rare thing is the relational work required to make that coalition possible.

How we use the money

Deep circles. Intimate retreats, hosted dinners, and river walks for the 20–30 key leaders whose relationships are the keystone of everything else.

Broad engagement. Facilitated film and art experiences across Whatcom County — in theaters, grange halls, libraries, and schools — gathering voices for a community-wide visioning process.

The story itself. Film, photography, visual art, and spoken word — captured and reflected back to the community. Story is not decoration here. It is the infrastructure.

What it unlocks beyond water

The same social soil that makes a watershed agreement more durable also makes a community more resilient to economic disruption, ecological crisis, and the broader erosion of institutional trust.

Place-based relationship work of this kind is chronically underfunded precisely because its value radiates in every direction at once. Water is the organizing issue. The relationships are the asset.

The $52,000 invested in film production to date has already produced work with an estimated market value of $125,000. Year over year, this project has generated roughly $2 of impact for every $1 invested.
